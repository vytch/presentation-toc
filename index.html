The goal

Theory of constraints

The Theory of Constraints is a methodology for identifying the most important limiting factor (i.e. constraint) that stands in the way of achieving a goal and then systematically improving that constraint until it is no longer the limiting factor. In manufacturing, the constraint is often referred to as a bottleneck.

Bottleneck vs non-bottleneck

In a project

Bottleneck:

It is a resource whose capacity is less than or equal to the demand placed upon it.

Non-bottleneck

Any resource whose capacity is greater than the demand placed upon it

What makes a bottleneck

Ex: New to a technology

  • First time you use a framework
  • Then capacity of the FED is low

Ex: calculator

  • All the logic in the FED
  • Then the demand on the FED is high

It is not good or bad, it is the reality

What matters is how well your work on your bottlenecks

Why does it matter?

One hour wasted on a bottleneck is lost for the entire team.

One hour wasted by a non-bottleneck has little impact on the team.

Team delivery is limited by bottlenecks.

Improving your bottleneck will be more impactful than improving a non-bottleneck resource.

Working as a bottleneck

  • Expand capacity
  • Reduce demand

Extend capacity

  • Automation (Grunt / gulp)
  • Code scaffolding (yeoman)
  • Improve processes (excel to javascript converter)

Extend capacity (2)

  • Adding extra FEDs
  • Increase quality (architecture)
  • Training

QA design before FED implementation

If a non-bottleneck

Testers

BED

  • Simplify the HTML as much as possible

Content author

What we have learned

  • Individual efficiency vs team efficiency.
  • Taking constraints into account help to prioritize retro actions.
  • Bottlenecks move as the context changes: Re-assess.

Apply TOC in your projects

  • Identify the projects bottlenecks
  • Decide how to exploit them.
  • Subordinate every other decision to the bottleneck.
  • Elevate the bottleneck.
  • And again!
